## What's Happening in Coleraine, MN: Housing, Mining, and Community Notes

Coleraine sits in western Itasca County and has long lived in the shadow of the bigger Iron Range cities to the east. According to local reports, that low profile is exactly what is starting to attract buyers who have priced out of Grand Rapids and Hibbing. The town's housing stock, much of it built during the early mining era around Longyear Lake, continues to offer some of the most affordable entry points in northern Minnesota.

Recent data suggests Coleraine home prices remain well below the Itasca County median, with many properties trading in the $90,000 to $170,000 range. City records indicate that turnover in the older neighborhoods near the Greenway School complex has been steady, with buyers favoring updated 1920s and 1930s homes that still carry their original character. The local school district, Greenway, continues to anchor family demand. Reports from district communications point to stable enrollment and ongoing facility maintenance work, which matters for buyers weighing Coleraine against neighboring Bovey or Taconite.

On the employment side, mining remains the underlying economic story. According to industry coverage, the Hibbing Taconite and Minorca operations continue to drive paycheck stability for households across the western Iron Range, including Coleraine. Forestry contractors and small trades businesses round out the local job base. Tourism plays a smaller but real role, with Longyear Lake drawing summer activity and the Mesabi Trail bringing cyclists through town during the warmer months.

Local reports also note continued interest in the Coleraine waterfront. Longyear Lake properties, when they list, tend to move faster than the broader market. The lake's in-town location, walkable to the small downtown and the Coleraine City Hall area, makes it a different product from the remote cabin lakes north of Grand Rapids. Buyers looking for a year-round home with water access rather than a seasonal cabin often end up here.

Infrastructure-wise, city records indicate ongoing street and utility work consistent with what most older Iron Range communities are dealing with. Nothing dramatic, but worth tracking for buyers who want to know what a property's special assessments might look like in coming years.

For buyers and sellers in Coleraine, the practical takeaway is that this is a market that rewards patience and local knowledge. Knowing which blocks have original mining-era construction quality, which lots sit in lower-lying areas near the lake, and what Greenway School boundaries actually mean for resale all matter.
