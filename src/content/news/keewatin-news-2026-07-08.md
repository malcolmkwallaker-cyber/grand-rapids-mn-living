## Keewatin Update: Small Iron Range Community Continues to Draw Overlooked Value

Keewatin sits quietly between Nashwauk and Hibbing along U.S. Highway 169, and according to local reports, it remains one of the more overlooked housing markets on the western Mesabi Iron Range. With a population hovering just above 1,000 residents, the city has long been defined by its proximity to the Hibbing Taconite operation and the Keetac plant operated by U.S. Steel, which continues to anchor local employment.

Recent data suggests Keetac remains in steady production, and industry watchers have noted continued investment in Iron Range taconite operations as domestic steel demand holds. For a town the size of Keewatin, the stability of a single major employer matters more than headline economic figures. City records indicate the tax base has held steady, and local infrastructure spending has focused on street maintenance and utility upgrades rather than expansion projects.

On the community side, the Keewatin High School building, now serving as part of the Nashwauk-Keewatin district, remains a central piece of civic identity. Local reports point to consistent participation in district athletics and community events tied to the Mesabi Trail, which passes through the area and continues to attract cyclists and outdoor users during warmer months. The trail connection is a quiet asset that many buyers from outside the region do not factor in until they see it in person.

Housing stock in Keewatin skews older, with a large share of homes built during the mid-twentieth century mining boom. Recent data suggests median sale prices remain well below the state average, with many properties trading in ranges that would be unheard of in the Twin Cities metro or even in Duluth. That affordability is exactly why the town continues to draw attention from investors, first-time buyers, and out-of-state buyers looking for a foothold in northern Minnesota.

The honest read on Keewatin: it is not flashy, it is not growing quickly, and it does not need to. What it offers is affordable ownership, steady employment nearby, and access to the same lakes, forests, and trails that command far higher prices elsewhere. Buyers who understand the Iron Range know the numbers here are hard to argue with.
